So let me introduce this amazing young man to you, Mann Patel is a 21 years old Software engineer and a UI/UX designer and a member, more to say a captain of the international AI development team 'Bezos.backward' which secured National Rank 17 amongst 10,000+ registered participants in the Amazon Machine Learning Challenge. He also worked and studied at Google in Los Angeles, they call them “nooglers”.

I know. How many people’s dreams are based on working and being part of the Google team, but Mann already was at crazy age, him being 18, exactly after he finished his studies in Computer Science and Engineering at “Devang Patel University” in India. Now he studies for his Master at University of Southern California.

And in one of his earliest roles, Mann served as a machine learning researcher at his institute. There, he built a deepfake tweet generator that mimicked a user’s writing style based on their past tweets. He experimented with GPT-2 and RoBERTa-based models, comparing fine-tuned and custom-trained variants using the Giant Language Model Test Room (GLTR). The models achieved fooling rates of 61% (GPT-2) and 53% (custom), providing insight into adversarial generation and language manipulation.

Man… those Indians are something else right?

And that is not it. Remember what I said about him winning the hackathon alone? He went to one of the biggest Hackathons there were in that period, and I’m talking about HackSC 2024 where Mann took first place in the Artists Track with his project FontSearch. He tackled a real-world problem and turned it into something impressive enough to stand out in a competitive field.

His project, "font_search," is a semantic search engine designed to transform the way fonts are found and created. By simply describing the desired style or mood in natural language, users can find matching fonts or even generate entirely new ones. The system bridges creative intent with computational design.

The development journey began with a key challenge:

The lack of an accessible, detailed font dataset. To overcome this, Mann scraped thousands of free fonts from dafonts.com and wrote a Python script to render over 13,000 fonts into images containing the phrase “The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog,” alongside all alphanumeric characters. These images were processed using the Google Gemini Flash model, which generated structured semantic data for each font. Each font was annotated with fields such as: detailed description, personality, practical use, cultural intuition, and search keywords forming the core document base for the engine.
